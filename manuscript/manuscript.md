The Role of Tibetan Plateau Topography in Shaping the Southern Hemisphere Zonal Wave 3

Yu Zhao1, Xupeng Wang1, and Ziyu Yan1

1School of Geoscience and Technology, Zhengzhou University, Zhengzhou, China

Key Points:

- Tibetan Plateau forcing excites a significant cross-hemispheric Zonal Wave 3 train in austral winter via Indian Ocean coupling.
- A negative dipole-like Indian Ocean pattern, driven by asymmetric wind forcing and ocean memory, bridges this teleconnection.
- Vertical baroclinic energy conversion via deep convection maintains the deep quasi-stationary Zonal Wave 3 structure.

Abstract

As the "Third Pole," the Tibetan Plateau (TP) profoundly influences Northern Hemisphere climate, yet its cross-equatorial impacts on Southern Hemisphere (SH) mid- to high-latitude circulation remain unclear. Using topographic sensitivity experiments in the fully coupled Community Earth System Model version 2 (CESM2), we investigate the teleconnection between TP forcing and the SH Zonal Wave 3 (ZW3) pattern. Results indicate that TP forcing excites a significant, robust, quasi-stationary ZW3 wave train during the austral winter (JJA) with a deep equivalent barotropic structure. Mechanism diagnoses reveal the TP shapes a distinct "negative IOD-like" sea surface temperature (SST) dipole in the tropical Indian Ocean via an asymmetric mechanism: a western cold anomaly actively driven by the enhanced Somali Jet, and an eastern warm anomaly maintained by pre-monsoon (MAM) oceanic thermal inertia. This SST dipole drives a deep zonal overturning circulation, generating an upper-tropospheric divergence dipole that acts as a Rossby wave source. Wave activity flux and energetics analyses confirm the wave train propagates southeastward into the SH westerly waveguide, maintained by extracting available potential energy from the background flow primarily through vertical baroclinic energy conversion () associated with deep moist convection. This study establishes a "TP–Indian Ocean–Deep Convection–ZW3" pathway, expanding the global perspective of TP climate effects and providing a mechanistic understanding of the interhemispheric drivers of Southern Hemisphere circulation variability.

**Plain Language Summary**

The Tibetan Plateau (TP) profoundly influences Northern Hemisphere climate, yet its cross-equatorial impact on Southern Hemisphere (SH) mid- to high-latitude circulation remains unclear. This study examines how TP topography modulates the SH Zonal Wave 3 (ZW3) pattern. Simulations using the Community Earth System Model (CESM) show that the TP exerts a significant influence on ZW3 during the austral winter. Mechanism analysis reveals that this teleconnection is intimately linked to a "negative IOD-like" sea surface temperature (SST) dipole in the Indian Ocean, which is shaped by the TP via an asymmetric driving mechanism. This asymmetric SST mode drives deep atmospheric convection, thereby exciting a teleconnection wave train. Furthermore, energy analysis indicates that this wave train maintains its deep structure primarily through vertical energy conversion processes associated with deep moist convection. These findings underscore the importance of considering the thermal states of the TP and the Indian Ocean to fully understand the dynamics of Southern Hemisphere climate variability.

1 Introduction

As a dominant atmospheric circulation mode in the Southern Hemisphere (SH) mid-latitudes, the Zonal Wave 3 (ZW3) pattern manifests as a prominent mode in geopotential height, sea level pressure, and wind fields. It significantly modulates the SH mid-latitude zonally asymmetric circulation on sub-monthly, seasonal, and interannual timescales (Hansen & Sutera, 1991; Mo & Ghil, 1987; Mo & White, 1985; Raphael, 2004; Renwick, 2005; Trenberth, 1980; van Loon & Jenne, 1972). ZW3 is primarily characterized by a quasi-stationary wave structure consisting of three alternating high-pressure ridges and low-pressure troughs in the mid-to-high latitudes. Its maximum amplitude occurs near 55°S, exhibiting a distinct barotropic structure (van Loon & Jenne, 1972; Goyal et al., 2022). While present in the annual mean, ZW3 exhibits a pronounced seasonal cycle: it is strongest and situated westward during the austral winter (JJA), and weakest and shifted eastward during the austral summer (DJF) (van Loon & Jenne, 1972; Turner et al., 2017). Previous studies indicate that due to its unique asymmetric structure, ZW3 profoundly influences meridional heat and momentum transport in SH mid-to-high latitudes. Consequently, ZW3 is intimately linked to variations in temperature and sea ice (Cerrone & Fusco, 2018; Raphael, 2007; Raphael & Hobbs, 2014; Yuan & Li, 2008). Notably, ZW3 activity played a role in the unprecedented decline of Antarctic sea ice during the spring of 2016 (Purich & England, 2019; Wang et al., 2019). ZW3 significantly affects SH mid-latitude frontal systems (Boschat et al., 2023), and its phase and amplitude are closely correlated with mid-latitude precipitation anomalies over South America (Ortiz-Guzmán et al., 2024). Additionally, ZW3 is intrinsically related to the asymmetric component of the Southern Annular Mode (SAM), profoundly affecting the climate through its coupling with the SAM (Campitelli et al., 2022; Fogt & Marshall, 2020). From polar sea ice to the frontal systems and precipitation that impact mid-latitude populations, the influence of ZW3 on SH climate is becoming increasingly prominent.

Given the unique structure and climatic significance of ZW3, its underlying mechanisms have been the subject of extensive research in recent years. Early studies generally attributed the formation of ZW3 to the zonal asymmetry of the three landmasses alternating with oceans in the SH mid-latitudes (Raphael, 1998; van Loon & Jenne, 1972). However, recent research indicates that ZW3 is driven by zonally asymmetric deep atmospheric convection in the tropics (Goyal et al., 2021). The Indo-Pacific warm pool plays a crucial role in generating the stationary ZW3, shifting the focus of mechanistic studies from the SH high latitudes to the tropics. Furthermore, Wang et al. (2023), using coupled model sensitivity experiments with and without the Tibetan Plateau (TP), demonstrated that the TP can indirectly influence Antarctica via stationary waves forced by Indian Ocean sea surface temperature (SST), ultimately forming a circum-Antarctic Rossby wave train. This finding extends the perspective of SH circulation mechanisms to cross-hemispheric teleconnection processes, in which the TP may play a key role.

With its unique geographical location, significant elevation, and thermal characteristics, the TP is known as the "Third Pole" of the Earth and exerts a profound influence on global climate through mechanical and thermal forcing (Wu et al., 2007, 2015; Yeh et al., 1957). On a regional scale, the mechanical and thermal forcing of the TP not only drives the onset of the Asian monsoon and accelerates the Somali Jet but also significantly modulates the atmospheric water cycle and its interaction with the Indo-Pacific warm pool (Huang et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2012). On a hemispheric scale, the TP acts as a key source of stationary Rossby waves, remotely modulating the North Pacific westerly jet, atmospheric rivers, and oceanic memory processes via thermal forcing (Hoskins & Karoly, 1981; Sun et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2024). The TP not only significantly modulates regional and Northern Hemisphere (NH) climate but also exerts far-reaching impacts on the global climate system through atmospheric circulation and air-sea coupling processes (Liu et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2012). While the mechanisms underlying the TP's impact on NH climate have been well clarified, research on its influence on SH climate remains relatively scarce. This is largely because the presence of the equatorial easterly barrier impedes the cross-equatorial propagation of atmospheric circulation anomalies generated by NH climate factors.

Nevertheless, studies indicate that the ocean may play a pivotal role in transmitting the climatic impacts of the TP across hemispheres (Wang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021). The Indian Ocean, given its unique cross-equatorial location and SST consistency, plays a distinct role in these cross-hemispheric linkages, particularly through its Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD) mode (Gillett et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2019). The Indian Ocean not only modulates global circulation patterns such as the Southern Annular Mode (Dhame et al., 2020; Hou et al., 2024) but also responds significantly to the thermal forcing of the TP through adjustments in the local Walker and Hadley circulations (Zhao et al., 2021; Sharma et al., 2022). These profound cross-hemispheric impacts are highly consistent with the assessment of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC, 2022). The report emphasizes that physical processes in "Third Pole" regions, including the TP, can profoundly influence weather and climate patterns in mid-to-low latitudes via atmospheric teleconnections and the ocean meridional overturning circulation, potentially triggering cascading global risks. Encouragingly, recent years have seen increased research on the interaction between the TP and SH atmospheric circulation patterns (Dou et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2022, 2024). However, these studies have predominantly focused on the Antarctic Oscillation (AAO), with insufficient attention paid to sub-dominant modes such as ZW3, let alone their underlying mechanisms.

Goyal et al. (2021) provided a novel perspective on ZW3 generation driven by tropical convection, while Wang et al. (2023) revealed the critical role of Indian Ocean SST in the cross-hemispheric forcing of the TP. However, what are the spatiotemporal characteristics of the stationary ZW3 excited by TP forcing? Through what specific dynamic mechanisms does the TP exert a profound influence on the SH ZW3? In light of this, this study investigates the seasonal variations of the TP's impact on the ZW3 teleconnection and conducts an in-depth analysis of the austral winter (JJA), when the impact is most pronounced. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 details the climate model used, the design of the sensitivity experiments, and the validation of the model's ability to simulate ZW3. Section 3 describes the ZW3 index definition and physical diagnostic methods employed. Section 4 presents the main results: first characterizing the seasonal features and vertical structure of the ZW3 response to TP forcing (Section 4.1); then elucidating how the TP modulates Indian Ocean SST via wind-induced evaporation and latent heat feedback (Section 4.2), and how these SST anomalies excite a Rossby wave source through air-sea coupling (Section 4.3); and finally reconstructing the dynamic path of wave train propagation across hemispheres and its maintenance mechanism in the SH via wave activity flux and energy conversion diagnoses (Section 4.4). Finally, Section 5 provides the conclusions and discussion.

2 Model and experimental design

2.1 Model description

Numerical simulations in this study were performed using the Community Earth System Model version 2.1.2 (CESM2.1.2). Developed primarily by the National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCAR), CESM2 is a state-of-the-art, fully coupled global climate modeling system widely used in climate research (see https://www.cesm.ucar.edu/models/cesm2 for details). CESM2 simulates Earth's past, present, and future climate states, and its reproduction of the 20th-century climate shows good agreement with observations (Danabasoglu et al., 2020). The model comprises four core physical components—atmosphere, land, ocean, and sea ice—which exchange fluxes in real time via the Common Infrastructure for Modeling the Earth (CIME) (Danabasoglu et al., 2020). Specifically, the atmospheric component is the Community Atmosphere Model version 6 (CAM6) (Danabasoglu et al., 2020); the land component is the Community Land Model version 5 (CLM5) (Lawrence et al., 2019); the ocean component is the Parallel Ocean Program version 2 (POP2) (Danabasoglu et al., 2020; Smith et al., 2010); and the sea ice component is the Community Ice CodE version 5 (CICE5) (Hunke et al., 2015).

The model was configured using the standard f09_g17 grid system, corresponding to a nominal horizontal resolution of approximately 1°. CAM6 and CLM5 share the same horizontal grid (0.9° × 1.25°). CAM6 features 32 vertical levels with a model top at 2 hPa, while CLM5 includes 16 vertical soil layers. POP2 utilizes a displaced-pole grid with a horizontal resolution of 1.25° × (0.27°–0.54°), where the meridional resolution is finest near the equator (~0.27°) and increases toward the poles. It is configured with 60 vertical levels. CICE5 shares the same horizontal grid as POP2 and includes 8 sea-ice layers and 3 snow layers vertically, utilizing 5 ice thickness categories.

2.2 Experimental design

To investigate the teleconnection between the TP and ZW3, we designed and conducted two fully coupled experiments. Both simulations utilized pre-industrial CO2 concentrations (285 ppm) and standard physical parameterization schemes. First, a control experiment (CTRL) was performed with the original TP elevation. It underwent a long spin-up integration until the model reached a quasi-equilibrium state. Following the spin-up (defined as model year 1850), the CTRL run was integrated for another 200 years (model years 1850–2049) with the original topography. A sensitivity experiment (SENS) was branched from the CTRL run at the same time (model year 1850). In this experiment, the topography within the TP region (25°N–45°N, 60°E–110°E) was reduced to 5% of its original height (Figure S1). The SENS run was also integrated synchronously for 200 years (model years 1850–2049). To ensure reasonable land-atmosphere feedbacks following the topographic adjustment, surface boundary conditions in the modified region were reconstructed. Features such as vegetation, snow cover, and glaciers were spatially interpolated based on the surrounding areas. To ensure the data reached a stable state, the last 100 years (model years 1950–2049) of each experiment were selected for analysis. The topographic effects of the TP were obtained by calculating the difference between the CTRL and SENS fields. Regarding the temporal scale, we first preliminarily explored seasonal variability. The analysis then focused on the austral winter (JJA) mean characteristics to elucidate the mechanisms, as the impact is most pronounced during this season.

3 Data and Method

3.1 Data

To evaluate the capability of CESM2.1.2 in simulating ZW3, we utilized the fifth generation ECMWF atmospheric reanalysis (ERA5) dataset (Hersbach et al., 2020). The data covers the period from 1979 to 2022 at a monthly temporal resolution and a horizontal resolution of 0.25° × 0.25°. We employed the ZW3 index proposed by Goyal et al. (2022). The model's performance was validated by comparing the regression of this index onto 500-hPa meridional wind anomalies against observations. Results indicate that CESM2.1.2 reproduces the key features of ZW3 well (Figure S2). Specifically, the explained variance of the first EOF mode (EOF1) in the model is 22.7%, which closely matches the 22.1% in ERA5. The variance of the second mode (EOF2) is 20.3% in the model, comparable to the 16.9% in ERA5. The spatial patterns of these leading EOF modes in CESM (Figures S2a, b) exhibit high consistency with ERA5 (Figures S2c, d). Notably, the centers of the strongest meridional wind anomalies coincide with observed locations, particularly in the Pacific and Atlantic sectors. Furthermore, Eabry et al. (2024) verified the credibility of CESM2 by assessing its simulation of ZW3 and the Southern Annular Mode (SAM). Goyal et al. (2022) also provided a detailed comparison between CESM2 and ERA5 regarding ZW3. Collectively, these findings confirm that CESM2.1.2 is a reliable tool for simulating ZW3.

3.2 Method

3.2.1 Definition of ZW3 Indices

To rigorously identify whether the stationary waves excited by TP forcing exhibit significant ZW3 characteristics and to quantitatively evaluate the intensity of this forced response, this study comprehensively employs three classic ZW3 indices originally designed to describe internal variability. These include the index first defined by Mo and White (1985) based on three specific locations; the improved index by Raphael (2004) based on optimized locations; and the most recent index proposed by Goyal et al. (2022) based on Empirical Orthogonal Functions (EOF). Although these indices were originally developed to characterize internal variability, applying them to diagnose forced responses (similar to indices like the NAO or SAM) is a standard practice in climate science (Arblaster & Meehl, 2006; Christiansen et al., 2022), aligning with established standards in the field of climate detection and attribution (Hegerl et al., 2010).

In the calculation of these indices, the forced response is defined as the systematic signal driven by the alteration of the external boundary condition (TP topography), specifically the difference between the time means of the Control (CTRL) and Sensitivity (SENS) experiments. The internal variability refers to spontaneous fluctuations generated by the climate system under fixed boundary conditions. This represents the inherent statistical characteristics of ZW3 in its undisturbed state (CTRL), including its fluctuation amplitude and dominant spatial modal structure in key regions. By normalizing the forced response or projecting it onto this internal variability baseline, we quantify the relative intensity and significance of the TP-forced ZW3 signal against the background internal variability.

Regarding the indices defined based on three specific locations, Mo and White (1985) first proposed coordinates at (50°S, 95°E), (58°S, 150°W), and (38°S, 15°W). Raphael (2004) subsequently improved these to points along 49°S at 50°E, 166°E, and 76°W. Both indices are calculated using the 500-hPa geopotential height field. 3.2.3 Statistical Significance Test

To ensure that the ZW3 forced response signals and dynamic diagnostic results identified in this study are not artifacts of the internal stochastic noise of the climate system, we employed standard statistical testing procedures (Wilks, 2011). regarding sample construction, interannual seasonal means were first calculated for all diagnostic variables. Subsequently, independent interannual time series with a sample size of 100 were generated corresponding to the 100-year integration period, thereby satisfying the fundamental assumptions required for parametric tests. To test the significance of the systematic response induced by TP forcing (defined as the difference between CTRL and SENS), a Student's t-test was applied. This test verifies whether the long-term mean of the 100 interannual difference samples is statistically significantly different from zero. This testing procedure was applied to evaluate both the changes in ZW3 indices and the spatial distribution of significance for two-dimensional physical fields.

4 Results

4.1 Spatiotemporal Characteristics of the ZW3 Forced Response

To identify the atmospheric circulation response characteristics excited by Tibetan Plateau (TP) topographic forcing, we first examine the geopotential height difference fields between the Control (CTRL) and Sensitivity (SENS) experiments across different seasons. As shown in Figures 1a–d, the SH circulation anomalies induced by TP forcing exhibit strong seasonality in the 500-hPa geopotential height field. In DJF (Figure 1a) and MAM (Figure 1b), the circulation response in high latitudes is relatively weak and spatially disorganized. However, the circulation response significantly strengthens in JJA (Figure 1c), presenting a clear ZW3 pattern within the high-latitude westerly belt (40°S–70°S). The wave train consists of three alternating high-pressure ridges and low-pressure troughs. Notably, the positions of the ridge centers align more closely with the classic ZW3 activity centers defined in the literature (Mo & White, 1985; Raphael, 2004) compared to other seasons, morphologically confirming the excitation of the ZW3 mode by TP forcing. In SON (Figure 1d), although a certain amplitude is maintained, the spatial organization is far less distinct than in JJA.

To further reveal the dynamical properties of this forced response, Figures 1e–h display the vertical profiles of geopotential height stationary wave deviations in the core ZW3 waveguide region (55°S). The results indicate that the forced response in JJA (Figure 1g) possesses a typical equivalent barotropic structure extending from near the surface to the lower stratosphere, exhibiting a clear ZW3 pattern. The phases of the ridges and troughs remain highly consistent in the vertical direction, while the amplitude increases significantly with height, peaking at approximately 200 hPa. In contrast, results for other seasons show poorer spatial organization or weaker amplitudes. Since JJA exhibits a deep dynamic standing wave penetrating the entire troposphere, subsequent physical mechanism diagnoses in this study will focus on JJA, where the response is most significant, with a particular emphasis on 200 hPa.

As an early pioneering work, the key points defined by Mo & White (1985) were constrained by data limitations at the time, resulting in a large latitudinal span. In contrast, Raphael (2004) used a longer period of reanalysis data to spatially optimize and correct the ZW3 activity centers, focusing them more on the zonal waveguide core region near 49°S. Therefore, the higher significance of the Raphael index indicates that the ZW3 response excited by TP forcing spatially aligns highly with this compact classic ZW3 mode. Furthermore, it is worth noting that the Mo & White index shows a significant negative response in JJA, whereas the Raphael index shows a significant positive response. This sign difference precisely corroborates the wave phase structure in Figure 1c: the key points of the Mo & White index are primarily located in the trough regions of the ZW3, while the points of the Raphael index precisely locate the ridge centers. This physical consistency effectively rules out the randomness of the index definitions and confirms that what the TP forcing excites is indeed the classic ZW3 standing wave mode with a fixed phase.

Second, the EOF-based diagnostics (Figures 2c–d) reveal the projection efficiency and phase locking of the TP-forced response. Regarding magnitude () (Figure 2d), the response is highly significant in all seasons except DJF. Notably, the magnitude in JJA is exceptionally high. The contributing PC1 and PC2 components share the same sign and exhibit extremely high significance, implying that the TP-forced wave train structure in JJA is spatially highly robust. Regarding phase () (Figure 2c), excluding DJF due to the high phase uncertainty resulting from its weak amplitude, the results demonstrate a unique phase shift induced by TP forcing in JJA. Compared to the average phase near 0° in MAM and SON, JJA exhibits a phase shift of approximately 60°. In summary, both the EOF-based diagnostics and the point-based index analysis jointly confirm that TP topography exerts the strongest and most robust excitation on the ZW3 mode during the JJA season.

4.2 TP Modulation of Indian Ocean SST

To investigate the initiation of the TP-ZW3 teleconnection, we first examined the thermal impact of TP topographic forcing on the upstream Indian Ocean, a key region. Figure 3a displays the absolute difference in sea surface temperature (SST) between the Control (CTRL) and Sensitivity (SENS) experiments during JJA. The results indicate that TP forcing excites a dipole-type "negative IOD-like" SST anomaly in the tropical Indian Ocean. This pattern is characterized by significant negative anomalies in the western tropical Indian Ocean and positive anomalies in the east. This finding is consistent with the surface air temperature (SAT) changes following TP removal reported by Wang et al. (2023), thereby validating the reasonableness of our experimental design. To isolate the seasonal specificity of this dipole mode by removing the annual mean background, Figure 3c presents the JJA seasonal SST deviation relative to the annual mean. The results demonstrate that after removing the annual mean climatological difference, the "west-cold, east-warm" structure in the tropical Indian Ocean remains distinct and becomes even more pronounced. This strongly confirms that the "negative IOD-like" mode is a unique forced response specific to the JJA season, rather than a systematic residual of the background climatology or a mere continuation of the annual mean signal.

This enhanced low-level wind field explains part of the tropical Indian Ocean SST anomalies associated with the "negative IOD-like" mode shown in Figure 3.

Driven by wind stress, cold water near the coast of the Arabian Sea upwells strongly to the surface and subsequently cools the western Indian Ocean surface via advection.

While the aforementioned dynamic and thermodynamic processes perfectly explain the cold anomaly in the western tropical Indian Ocean associated with the "negative IOD-like" mode, they fail to fully account for the warm anomaly in the east. As shown in Figures 5a and 5b, within the warm SST center in the eastern tropical Indian Ocean (refer to Figure 3a), neither  nor  exhibits strong positive values sufficient to maintain this warm anomaly; instead, weak negative values are observed in some areas. This discrepancy suggests that the warm SSTs in this region are likely not generated by concurrent surface heat fluxes in JJA. Supplementary experimental analysis (Figure S3) attributes this warm anomaly primarily to oceanic "thermal inertia" or the "ocean memory" mechanism (Deser et al., 1996). As shown in Figure S3a, significant strong warm anomalies had already accumulated in this region during the pre-monsoon season (MAM). Although the  tendency in JJA acts as a cooling damping factor (Figure S3b), the JJA SST reconstructed based on Hasselmann's (1976) stochastic climate model theory—where SST is viewed as the time integration of atmospheric forcing—(Figure S3d) bears high resemblance to the original results (Figure S3c) in key regions. This confirms that the strong initial warm anomalies accumulated in MAM are sustained through the integration effect. This phenomenon is strikingly similar to the contrasting responses of the Indian Ocean to TP thermal forcing before and after the South Asian monsoon onset explored by Zhao et al. (2021). Furthermore, this mechanism aligns with the SST decay process in the "Indian Ocean Capacitor effect," where the ocean maintains anomalies via thermal inertia even after the external forcing has subsided or reversed (Du et al., 2009; Xie et al., 2009). Therefore, the warm SST anomaly in the eastern Indian Ocean is primarily a cross-seasonal persistence of pre-existing thermal memory via oceanic thermal inertia, rather than being generated by concurrent atmospheric forcing. This demonstrates significant integration effects and capacitor-like characteristics.

In summary, TP topographic forcing shapes a unique dipole-type "negative IOD-like" SST mode in the Indian Ocean via an "east-west asymmetric" dynamic-thermodynamic coupling mechanism. In the western Indian Ocean (the active forcing zone), the TP-excited Somali Jet actively generates significant cold SST anomalies through the dual cooling effects of primary thermal evaporation (Figure 5) and secondary dynamic upwelling and advection (Figure 4). Conversely, in the eastern Indian Ocean (the passive maintenance zone), SST anomalies originate primarily from the cross-seasonal persistence of thermal memory from the preceding season, rather than concurrent atmospheric forcing. This Indian Ocean SST mode, directly shaped by TP forcing and characterized by a significant zonal gradient, constitutes the critical thermal boundary condition for launching the atmospheric wave train toward the Southern Hemisphere.

4.3 Air-Sea Coupling Response and Excitation of the Rossby Wave Source

Section 4.2 confirmed that TP topographic forcing establishes a distinct "negative IOD-like" SST anomaly dipole characterized by "cold-west, warm-east" conditions in the Indian Ocean. This section further elucidates how this sea surface thermodynamic boundary condition drives large-scale vertical circulation adjustments via air-sea coupling processes and excites a Rossby wave source in the upper troposphere, thereby completing the physical transition from thermal signals to dynamic waves.

SST anomalies directly modulate tropical convective activity by altering the distribution of diabatic heating at the air-sea interface. As shown in Figure 6a, Outgoing Longwave Radiation (OLR) anomalies exhibit response characteristics highly consistent with the SST anomalies in Figure 3a. The eastern tropical Indian Ocean (warm water region) displays significant negative OLR anomalies, indicating intensely enhanced deep convection. Conversely, the western region (cold water region) displays positive OLR anomalies, indicating suppressed convection. This "east-wet, west-dry" thermal response is further corroborated by the total precipitation rate (PRECT) field (Figure 6b). The significant increase in monsoon precipitation over the eastern Indian Ocean implies a substantial release of latent heat energy into the atmosphere in this region. As shown in Figures 6d–f, this zonal asymmetry in diabatic heating drives strong adjustments in the vertical circulation. Analysis of the vertical velocity ($\omega$) reveals that the spatial distributions at 200 hPa (Figure 6d) and 500 hPa (Figure 6e) bear high similarity to the OLR and precipitation fields, indicating the deep penetrating nature of the convection. Furthermore, the spatial consistency between precipitation anomalies (Figure 6b) and mid-tropospheric vertical velocity (Figure 6e) confirms that the induced circulation is primarily driven by diabatic heating associated with deep convection. This implies that the latent heat released by enhanced monsoon precipitation acts as a potent energy source, stimulating vertical ascending motion that links surface forcing to upper-level circulation. Specifically, the vertical profile (Figure 6f) displays a deep zonal overturning circulation structure over the tropical Indian Ocean: the east (~85°E–120°E) features a strong ascent zone penetrating the entire troposphere, while the west (~50°E–85°E) features a deep descent zone. This "west-descending, east-ascending" vertical motion dipole constitutes a key vertical channel connecting sea surface thermal forcing with the upper-atmospheric circulation.

Vertical motion anomalies inevitably necessitate dynamic adjustments in the horizontal divergence field to maintain atmospheric mass conservation. Figure 6c presents the vertical profile of meridional mean divergence in the tropical-subtropical region (25°N–25°S), reflecting the atmospheric mass redistribution driven by the Indian Ocean SST dipole. Its structure exhibits high physical consistency with the aforementioned thermal and vertical motion fields. Over the warm water region in the eastern Indian Ocean, the divergence field exhibits a significant baroclinic structure, characterized by convergence in the lower levels and strong divergence in the upper levels. This "low-level convergence, upper-level divergence" configuration corresponds to the deep convective precipitation (Figure 6b) and the column-integrated ascending motion (Figure 6f), indicating that tropical deep convection vertically transports low-level converged warm, moist air to the tropopause, where it flows outward. Conversely, over the cold water region in the western Indian Ocean, the pattern is reversed, showing "low-level divergence, upper-level convergence." Corresponding to the sinking motion in Figure 6f, the upper troposphere here exhibits a significant convergence center, indicating the convergence of high-altitude mass to compensate for the subsiding airflow.

Collectively, the "negative IOD-like" IOSSTA drives a deep "west-descending, east-ascending" zonal overturning circulation by modifying the diabatic heating distribution within the troposphere. While this vertical mass transport manifests as a "west-diverging, east-converging" divergence dipole at the bottom of the troposphere, under the influence of the "west-descending, east-ascending" vertical velocity anomalies, it ultimately transforms into a strong "west-converging, east-diverging" divergence dipole at the tropopause. This upper-level dynamic field anomaly, directly maintained by tropical diabatic heating, not only characterizes the direct atmospheric response to SST forcing but also provides the potential key initial dynamic perturbation for exciting the Rossby wave source via the vorticity stretching mechanism and launching the teleconnection wave train toward the SH high-latitude westerlies.

The upper-level divergence dipole revealed in Figure 6 is superimposed on the subtropical background absolute vorticity gradient. Through the vorticity stretching mechanism, it induces significant vorticity generation, dynamically functioning as a Rossby wave source (Sardeshmukh & Hoskins, 1988). As shown in Figure 7a, the 200-hPa horizontal divergence and divergent wind fields exhibit the structure of this dynamic source, further confirming the conclusions from the vertical divergence profile in Figure 6c. The strong divergence center (red) over the eastern Indian Ocean acts as the outflow region for deep convection, transporting divergent flow outward; meanwhile, the strong convergence center (blue) over the western Indian Ocean acts as the inflow region, absorbing surrounding air. Furthermore, significant divergent wind vectors surge from the eastern source region and converge into the western sink region. This intense non-uniformity in the horizontal flow field acts directly on the background flow, facilitating the formation of a cyclonic vorticity source over the tropical western Indian Ocean via strong convergence. Under the influence of this dynamic source, a clear quasi-stationary wave train emerges in the 200-hPa perturbation vorticity field (Figure 7c). Originating from the tropical Indian Ocean, the wave train manifests as alternating cyclonic and anticyclonic circulation anomaly centers arranged along a southeastward path. The ability of the wave train to maintain long-distance propagation and form a quasi-stationary structure is attributed to the favorable configuration of the atmospheric background flow, specifically the response characteristics of the 200-hPa zonal wind (Figure 7b). First, on a large scale, TP forcing induces a significant westerly acceleration belt in the tropical and subtropical regions. Second, on the wave scale, a train of positive and negative zonal wind anomaly centers appears clearly along this westerly waveguide. This feature aligns highly with the propagation path and phase structure of the vorticity wave train in Figure 7c. This indicates that the high spatial overlap between zonal wind anomalies and the perturbation vorticity wave train confirms the critical role of wave-flow interaction. Driven by the continuous excitation of the vorticity source by the tropical upper-level divergence dipole, the subtropical westerly jet enhanced by TP forcing acts as an anisotropic atmospheric waveguide. It not only constrains the meridional dispersion of wave energy but also effectively guides and maintains the long-distance propagation of the Rossby wave train from the tropical Indian Ocean source region to the SH high latitudes, ultimately completing the cross-hemispheric dynamic teleconnection process.

In summary, tropical Indian Ocean SST anomalies drive a deep "west-descending, east-ascending" zonal overturning circulation by modulating deep convection and latent heat release, forming a significant "west-converging, east-diverging" dynamic source region in the upper troposphere. Under the continuous action of this dynamic source, the Rossby wave train excited via the vorticity stretching mechanism is effectively trapped and guided by the intensified subtropical westerly waveguide, ultimately realizing the cross-hemispheric dynamic teleconnection from the tropical Indian Ocean to the SH high latitudes.

4.4 Propagation Path and Energetic Maintenance of the ZW3

To trace the energy propagation path of the Rossby wave train and verify its cross-hemispheric dynamic process, we first diagnosed the horizontal wave activity flux () as defined by Takaya and Nakamura (2001). As shown in Figure 8a, the 200-hPa wave activity flux difference field reveals an energy propagation channel extending from the Northern Hemisphere to the SH high latitudes. Initial wave energy is first excited upstream of the Tibetan Plateau due to topographic forcing but subsequently diminishes over the tropical Indian Ocean. Following a series of air-sea coupling responses in the tropical Indian Ocean, the originally weak wave activity flux is re-excited over the tropical southern Indian Ocean. Here, the  vectors converge and significantly strengthen, subsequently projecting southeastward into the SH subtropical westerly jet, and finally merging into the high-latitude waveguide. To precisely quantify the energy budget during this propagation process, we defined a parallelogram region extending along the wave train path (red dashed box in Figure 8a) as the spatial baseline for subsequent energetics diagnosis.

Figures 8b and 8c display the  vectors and meridional wind fields () for the Control (CTRL) and Sensitivity (SENS) experiments, respectively. Both experiments exhibit strong background westerlies in the SH high latitudes and possess certain wave characteristics, indicating that ZW3 is an intrinsic circulation mode inherent to the Southern Hemisphere. However, the critical difference lies in the energy transport channel from the tropics to the high latitudes. In the CTRL experiment (Figure 8b), the  vectors originating from the tropical southern Indian Ocean and directed southeastward are distinct. This re-excited energy flow merges directly into the westerly jet, significantly enhancing the intensity and coherence of the downstream wave train. In contrast, in the SENS experiment with TP topography removed (Figure 8c), although the background westerly waveguide remains, the critical  vectors originating from the tropical Indian Ocean are virtually absent. This results in a lack of sustained energy support from the tropics for the SH waves. This demonstrates that the TP, by modulating Indian Ocean SST and tropical convection, establishes a key energy maintenance mechanism in the tropical southern Indian Ocean. This mechanism successfully injects wave energy across the equator into the SH westerly waveguide, thereby significantly enhancing and maintaining the quasi-stationary Rossby wave train. However, considering the inevitable dissipation during long-distance propagation, further analysis from the perspective of local energy conversion is required.

To summarize, this section reconstructed the cross-hemispheric dynamic process of the ZW3 teleconnection wave train via wave activity flux and local energetics diagnoses. Regarding the propagation path, TP forcing first excites an initial perturbation dominated by vertical baroclinic energy conversion in the NH source region. Subsequently, under a series of air-sea coupling responses in the tropical Indian Ocean, the wave activity flux is significantly re-excited over the tropical southern Indian Ocean and transported southeastward by triggering transient eddy feedback and local baroclinic instability in the SH subtropics, eventually merging into the SH high-latitude westerly waveguide. Regarding wave maintenance, the TP-forced ZW3 maintains its significant quasi-stationary amplitude and equivalent barotropic structure by maintaining a specific phase tilt with the background thermal field and continuously extracting available potential energy from deep vertical heat flux release aided by moist convection processes.

5 Conclusions

In this study, using the CESM2.1.2 fully coupled climate model and designing topographic sensitivity experiments with (and without) the Tibetan Plateau (TP), we systematically investigated the cross-hemispheric teleconnection impact of TP topographic forcing on the Southern Hemisphere Zonal Wave 3 (ZW3) and its physical mechanisms. The main conclusions are summarized as follows.

First, the forced response of ZW3 to the TP exhibits distinct seasonal characteristics. In the austral winter (JJA), the TP excites the most significant and robust ZW3 teleconnection wave train. Vertically, this wave train presents a deep equivalent barotropic structure, and its spatial morphology and phase are comparable to the classic ZW3 mode defined by indices.

Second, the TP-ZW3 teleconnection in JJA demonstrates a unique physical mechanism, as illustrated in Figure 10.

1. TP topographic forcing shapes a significant "negative IOD-like" SST dipole in the tropical Indian Ocean through a synergistic effect dominated by thermodynamic processes and supplemented by dynamic processes. In the cold anomaly region of the western tropical Indian Ocean, TP forcing enhances the Somali Jet, causing significant surface cooling primarily through thermodynamic processes involving latent heat release, aided by the dynamic process of Ekman pumping. In contrast, the warm anomaly region in the eastern tropical Indian Ocean mainly originates from thermal memory established before the monsoon onset (MAM) and is maintained cross-seasonally via oceanic thermal inertia.
2. The "negative IOD-like" SST dipole shaped by TP forcing drives a deep convective dipole and excites a wave source in the upper troposphere at 200 hPa. As shown in Figure 10, via air-sea coupling, the SST dipole drives a deep tropical zonal overturning circulation (west-descending, east-ascending) and a deep convection dipole (suppressed in the west, enhanced in the east). This process forms a "west-converging, east-diverging" divergence dipole at 200 hPa, acting as a critical Rossby wave source via the vorticity stretching mechanism. The wave source propagates from the westerly window in the western tropical Indian Ocean across the equator and southeastward along a series of zonal wind anomaly waveguides.
3. The TP-ZW3 teleconnection exhibits a unique propagation path and energy maintenance mechanism. Wave activity flux () diagnosis reveals that the TP initially triggers strong excitation upstream, which diminishes sharply near the equator due to the easterly barrier. Subsequently, a re-excited wave train is identified over the tropical southern Indian Ocean, which merges southeastward into the intrinsic SH westerly waveguide. Local energetics diagnosis indicates that, apart from the intense excitation shared by barotropic/baroclinic conversions upstream of the TP, this teleconnection wave train is maintained primarily by baroclinic energy conversion (), specifically dominated by vertical baroclinic energy conversion () associated with deep moist convection. By maintaining a specific phase tilt with the background thermal field in SH high latitudes, the wave continuously extracts available potential energy from deep vertical heat flux release aided by moist convection, thereby maintaining its significant quasi-stationary amplitude and equivalent barotropic structure.

**Figure 10**. Schematic diagram of the dynamic mechanism by which TP forcing maintains the SH ZW3 mode. The physical process consists of three phases. (1) Surface Forcing: TP forcing induces an enhanced Somali Jet (red arrow, Figure 4), driving thermodynamic processes dominated by latent heat via evaporative cooling (Figure 5), and establishing a "negative IOD-like" SST mode (red dashed box, Figure 3) in the tropical Indian Ocean. (2) Vertical Coupling: SST anomalies excite a significant convective dipole (Figure 6a), manifested as opposite OLR anomalies in the eastern/western tropical Indian Ocean. This convective anomaly drives an anomalous zonal overturning circulation (Figures 6d–f), characterized by deep ascent (blue arrow) over the warm eastern Indian Ocean and deep descent (orange arrow) over the cold western Indian Ocean. This process establishes a dynamic source region of divergence and convergence in the upper troposphere (Figure 6c), generating divergent winds flowing from east to west (Figure 7a). (3) Teleconnection and Maintenance: The upper-level divergence source excites a Rossby wave train (Figure 7c), with the black thick arrow indicating the propagation path of wave activity flux (). The wave train propagates southward and merges into the SH westerlies (Figure 8). Along the path, baroclinic energy conversion (, red/blue circles) continuously converts available potential energy into kinetic energy (Figure 9), thereby resisting dissipation and maintaining the deep equivalent barotropic structure of the ZW3 mode (right panel, Figure 1g).

Therefore, this study attempts to synthesize the concept of "TP influencing Antarctica via the Indian Ocean" proposed by Wang et al. (2023) with the mechanism of "ZW3 excitation by tropical convection" proposed by Goyal et al. (2021). Our goal is to elucidate the complete physical process ranging from topographic forcing to the SH circulation response. Notably, both Wang et al. (2023) and Goyal et al. (2021) demonstrated the critical roles of the Indian Ocean or tropical convection through a series of rigorous AGCM sensitivity experiments. Work by Wang et al. (2023) confirmed the indispensable role of sea surface temperatures (SST), particularly the tropical Indian Ocean SST dipole, in transmitting TP signals across hemispheres. Their fixed-SST experiments indicated that specific Indian Ocean SST patterns are a prerequisite for the cross-hemispheric excitation of Rossby wave trains by TP signals. Similarly, Goyal et al. (2021) not only established the excitation effect of tropical convection on ZW3 via idealized experiments but also further pointed out, using realistic control experiments, that deep convection in the Indo-Pacific warm pool plays a significant role in generating stationary ZW3. These findings provide critical dynamic premises for the current study, enabling us to conduct research using fully coupled experiments that include complete air-sea feedbacks. This approach complements previous AGCM experiments, jointly constructing a panoramic view of the ZW3 cross-hemispheric teleconnection driven by topographic forcing via Indian Ocean SSTs and deep convection.

Furthermore, the "negative IOD-like" Indian Ocean SSTs identified in this study are crucial for the TP-forced ZW3. This corresponds to the "significant negative correlation between IOD and ZWN3 intensity" found by Kim et al. (2024), implying a positive correlation between a "negative IOD-like" state and ZW3 intensity. Regarding the formation mechanism of these "negative IOD-like" SST anomalies, unlike previous studies that broadly attributed Indian Ocean SST responses to overall atmospheric forcing, our diagnosis reveals a spatial asymmetry. The western cold anomaly is actively driven by the concurrent Somali Jet, aligning with mainstream academic views. In contrast, the eastern warm anomaly primarily originates from oceanic thermal inertia memory established during the pre-monsoon season (MAM). This aligns with the perspective of Zhao et al. (2021), who noted that "the Indian Ocean response to TP thermal forcing is diametrically opposite before and after the monsoon onset." Compared to direct forcing by pure atmospheric processes, oceanic memory is a long-term process. The cross-seasonal warm anomaly in the east further corroborates the indispensable role of the tropical Indian Ocean in the TP's cross-hemispheric modulation of ZW3, acting either as a concurrent ocean bridge or a cross-seasonal capacitor. Crucially, we verified that the TP-forced mode is independent of ENSO variability, as the removal of TP topography induces no systematic shift in the ENSO state (Fig. S4). Regarding the dynamic maintenance mechanism of Rossby waves, traditional views often emphasize barotropic processes or horizontal baroclinicity. Through local energetics diagnosis, this study is the first to quantify the dominant contribution of vertical baroclinic energy conversion () from a "moist dynamics" perspective. We found that by maintaining a specific phase tilt with the background field, the ZW3 wave train effectively utilizes vertical heat fluxes associated with deep convective precipitation latent heat release to extract available potential energy. In the broader context of global climate change, recent years have seen fruitful research on the climatic effects of ZW3 (Boschat et al., 2023; Cerrone & Fusco, 2018; Eabry et al., 2024; Ortiz-Guzmán et al., 2024; Purich & England, 2019; Wang et al., 2019) and interactions between the Antarctic and the Northern Hemisphere (Dou et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2022, 2024; Wen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). However, research specifically addressing the TP cross-hemispheric teleconnection to ZW3 remains scarce. This is the focal point of the current study, which attempts to clarify the specific mechanisms and propose new insights.

Nevertheless, this study has certain limitations. We acknowledge that the results are based solely on 200-year integration sensitivity experiments using a single fully coupled model (CESM2). Although CESM2 demonstrates excellent performance in simulating SH mid- to high-latitude circulation, differences in convection parameterization schemes and air-sea coupling strengths across models may lead to uncertainties in response magnitude. To extract cross-hemispheric teleconnection signals with low signal-to-noise ratios from the noisy climate system, we employed an idealized experimental design with substantial topographic reduction. This forcing magnitude far exceeds realistic topographic variability; thus, it is unsuitable for quantitative attribution of realistic climate change and serves only to qualitatively reveal fundamental dynamic mechanisms. Therefore, future work should involve Multi-Model Ensemble (MME) simulations to further verify the universality of this teleconnection mechanism under different model physical frameworks.
